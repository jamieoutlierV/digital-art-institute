# 2026-03-09-mari-katayama-v-a-parasol-foundation-women-in-phot

> Mari Katayama's V&A Parasol Foundation commission signals how major institutions are expanding the boundaries of photography to encompass multidisciplinary, embodied, and craft-based practices — particularly from practitioners whose bodies and identities have been historically marginalised. For the digital arts field, this is significant as a counterpoint and complement: Katayama's work occupies the same conceptual territory as digital identity construction (the self as authored, assembled, performed) but insists on materiality, manual labour, and the irreducible specificity of the physical body. The commission also marks continued institutional momentum toward platforming non-Western, disabled, and women artists within legacy cultural infrastructure, raising questions about whether such frameworks genuinely redistribute power or absorb difference into existing prestige economies.

## CLA Analysis

**L1 — Surface**: L1 — Mari Katayama, a multidisciplinary Japanese artist, has been commissioned by the V&A's Parasol Foundation Women in Photography programme for a work titled 'tree of life'. This is a named institutional commission at a major global museum.

**L2 — Systemic**: L2 — The commission reflects institutional investment in diversifying whose bodies and experiences are centred in photography collections. The Parasol Foundation's dedicated women-in-photography strand signals structured funding pipelines for gender equity in the medium, while the V&A's selection of a disabled Japanese artist working across craft, prosthetics, and photography indicates a broadening of what 'photography' means within museum taxonomies.

**L3 — Worldview**: L3 — The framing assumes photography is a site of identity negotiation, not merely documentation. Katayama's practice — where handmade objects, the artist's body, and the camera interact — challenges the Western institutional tendency to separate craft from fine art and positions embodied, lived experience as authoritative knowledge. The 'women in photography' frame, while corrective, still operates within binary gender categories.

**L4 — Narrative**: L4 — The 'tree of life' title invokes one of humanity's oldest mythic structures — growth, interconnection, rootedness — mapped onto a body that has been surgically altered and self-authored through craft. This is a deep story about wholeness not as physical completeness but as creative assembly: the body as something made, not just given. It mirrors digital art's implicit myth that identity is constructed, but grounds it in flesh, thread, and material resistance.
