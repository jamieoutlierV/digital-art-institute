`skills/scout-editorial.md` · A(DAI) · Read by `pipeline/scout.py` at runtime.
The difference between noise and intelligence lives here.

---

## Centre of Gravity

The scout tracks digital arts across two axes simultaneously — and the tension between
them is itself the most important signal in the corpus.

**Axis 1: Institutional recognition.**
The zone where computational and networked practices meet serious curatorial, critical,
and market attention. Reference points: Art Basel (especially Zero 10, Art Unlimited,
Meridians), Frieze, Venice Biennale, Ars Electronica, ZKM, Serpentine, Rhizome, V&A.
Not because these institutions define the field — they don't — but because their
inclusions and exclusions reveal the field's power dynamics.

**Axis 2: Practitioner reality.**
The vast majority of digital art has never been institutionally named as such. Demo
scene coders, VJ collectives, Twitch streamers doing live generative performance,
game modders making machinima, bedroom producers making visual music, open-source
tool builders who are also artists — these practitioners often reject the "digital art"
label entirely, yet they are doing exactly what the field has always done.

The scout must hold both without collapsing one into the other.
The gap between what institutions recognise and what practitioners actually do
is not a problem to solve — it is the most important thing to map.

---

## What Digital Art Actually Is

Not a medium. A system of artistic production in which digital technologies play an
essential role in the conception, creation, performance, distribution, or experience
of the work. It runs from the early 1960s to the present and has never been one thing.

The field contains communities that have never spoken to each other. A plotter artist
from 1968 and a fxhash minter from 2022 are working in the same lineage and are
almost certainly unaware of each other. The scout's job is to surface both.

## The Full Map of Practice

### 1. Generative & Algorithmic Art
Artworks that emerge from code, rules, data, or autonomous systems.

*Institutionalised:* Vera Molnár, Frieder Nake, Georg Nees, Manfred Mohr (1960s–),
Casey Reas, Ben Fry (Processing), Refik Anadol, Tyler Hobbs, Dmitri Cherniak.
Art Basel's Zero 10 sector. Art Blocks, fxhash in gallery context.

*Non-institutionalised / parallel:* Creative coding communities (p5.js, openFrameworks,
TouchDesigner, Max/MSP). Demoscene — 40-year tradition of real-time algorithmic
visual art on constrained hardware (Demozoo, Pouet.net, Assembly, Revision demoparty).
Shader art (Shadertoy.com community). Competitive creative coding. Glitch artists
working with data bending and compression artefacts. Pixel artists working with
constraint as methodology. Generative music with visual output (Supercollider,
TidalCycles visual extensions).

### 2. Immersive & Experiential Art
Works that create spatial, multi-sensory environments.

*Institutionalised:* teamLab, Ryoji Ikeda, Carsten Nicolai, Olafur Eliasson (digital
components), Random International. Major festival commissions at Ars Electronica,
Lux Helsinki, Vivid Sydney.

*Non-institutionalised / parallel:* Projection mapping communities (VPT, MadMapper
user communities) — large global scene operating entirely outside gallery context,
primarily for events, architecture, and public space. Fulldome artists working in
planetariums. Laser show artists. Immersive experience designers who would not call
themselves artists. Escape room designers using interactive narrative.
Burning Man / festival art with sophisticated digital components.
Underground rave visual artists.

### 3. Live Digital Performance
Code, visuals, sound, and embodiment in real time.

*Institutionalised:* TOPLAP / Algorave (live coding — TidalCycles, Sonic Pi,
Supercollider), Sam Aaron, Alex McLean, Joanne Armitage. VJ culture crossing
into gallery (Pfadfinderel, Legoman). AI performance (Holly Herndon, Memo Akten).

*Non-institutionalised / parallel:* VJ culture — massive global scene predating the
gallery's interest by 20 years (Resolume, VDMX communities, VJ forums, Vidvortex).
Club visual artists who are not represented and do not want to be.
Twitch and YouTube Live generative/visual art streams. Virtual performers and
Vtubers using motion capture as performance medium. DIY circuit bending performance.
Noise music with live video synthesis. Laser harp and sensor instrument builders.
Live cinema. Hip-hop visual production (Hype Williams lineage — digital as film grammar).

### 4. Networked, Online & Web-Based Art
The network as material, not gallery.

*Institutionalised:* Jodi (Joan Heemskerk & Dirk Paesmans), Olia Lialina,
Cory Arcangel, Rafaël Rozendaal, Hito Steyerl, Trevor Paglen. Rhizome's
ArtBase. NFT art in gallery context (Beeple at Christie's, xcopy, Harm van den Dorpel).

*Non-institutionalised / parallel:* Web art communities that reject gallery entirely
(Neocities revival, personal web aesthetics, gif culture, ASCII art).
Meme artists — image macros as a genuine folk art tradition with sophisticated
visual grammar. Early internet aesthetics communities (Y2K aesthetic, webcore).
Fan art ecosystems — enormous volume of technically sophisticated digital image-making
that the art world has never engaged with seriously.
Social media native artists (Instagram, TikTok) using platform constraints as form.
Game modders making total conversion mods as artistic statements.
Machinima — film made inside game engines (pre-dates Hito Steyerl's machine vision work).
NFT communities operating entirely outside gallery validation (on-chain, fxhash,
long-form generative) — often explicitly anti-institutional.
Dark net art. Anonymous art. AI art communities on Midjourney/SD outside gallery frame.

### 5. Robotic, Sensorial & Machine-Integrated Art
Digital art extended into embodied, mechanical, environmental form.

*Institutionalised:* teamLab, Random International, Marshmallow Laser Feast,
Universal Everything. ZKM robotics tradition. Sougwen Chung (robotic drawing).

*Non-institutionalised / parallel:* Maker/hacker communities — Adafruit, Hackaday,
Arduino community — doing kinetic and interactive work that is functionally identical
to gallery robotic art, without the institutional frame. DIY synth builders creating
visual instruments. Furby modders and animatronics artists. Open-source prosthetics
artists. Biohackers using electronics as body art (implantable RFID art).
LED and smart textile artists in fashion and costume — technically sophisticated
but aligned with craft rather than fine art.

### 6. Hybrid & Post-Digital Practice
Digital processes permeating the entire creative pipeline.

*Institutionalised:* CNC sculpture, 3D printing as fine art, AI-assisted choreography
(Wayne McGregor / Google Arts & Culture collaborations), data-driven architecture
framed as art practice.

*Non-institutionalised / parallel:* Craft communities using CNC and laser cutting
who would not call their work digital art. 3D printing hobbyist communities making
sculptural objects. Digital fabrication for theatre and production design.
Motion capture used in games, not framed as art. Digital restoration and archiving
as a creative practice. Data visualisation as journalism or science — the boundary
with art practice is actively contested.

### 7. Sound, Music & Audiovisual Art
*(Often absent from visual-art-centric digital art histories — included here explicitly)*

*Institutionalised:* Oval, Autechre, Pan Sonic (digital materiality in music),
Holly Herndon, Mark Fell, Matmos, Ryoji Ikeda (sound as primary).
Electroacoustic music tradition (IRCAM, EMS). Iannis Xenakis (computer music pioneer).

*Non-institutionalised / parallel:* Electronic music producers working with
generative systems (modular synthesis — enormous community, entirely self-organised).
Circuit bending tradition. Chiptune — composing in hardware-constrained systems as art.
Net label culture (Bandcamp, SoundCloud as distribution for non-commercial
experimental music). Vaporwave, hyperpop — genre communities with distinct visual
and sonic aesthetics that are not discussed as art but function as such.
Gamelan synthesis. AI music outside gallery (Suno, Udio user artists).
Beat making communities — sampling as digital collage, technically sophisticated.

## What Counts as a Signal

- **Practitioner profiles** — artists, collectives, researchers in any part of the map above
- **New works and commissions** — exhibitions, releases, performances, publications
- **Institutional moves** — acquisitions, residencies, grants, curatorial appointments,
  festival selections (including non-gallery: demoparty prizes, Twitch milestones,
  open-source tool launches, community awards)
- **Critical and theoretical writing** — essays, papers, interviews that name, contest,
  or expand what digital art is and who makes it
- **Tool and platform releases** — new software, hardware, platforms built for or by artists,
  including open-source (p5.js releases, TouchDesigner updates, new Shadertoy features)
- **Historical recovery** — documentation of overlooked practitioners, archive projects,
  retrospectives, Wikipedia corrections, oral history projects
- **Community events** — demoparties, live coding events, Algorave, VJ festivals,
  modular synth meetups — signal even without institutional press coverage
- **Market signals** — significant sales, auction results, platform shifts, only when
  they reveal field dynamics, not price speculation
- **Cross-field moments** — when a practice from the non-institutional track gets
  named, claimed, or contested by institutional actors (this is a high-value signal)

## What to Ignore

### Hard exclusions
- Digital marketing, digital transformation, SaaS, fintech — "digital" meaning business
- NFT floor price tracking, crypto trading, token speculation with no art context
- AI productivity tools (Copilot, ChatGPT features) with no artistic framing
- SEO content farms, AI-generated article mills
- Celebrity tech news unrelated to art practice
- Press releases with no editorial content beyond announcement
- "10 artists you need to follow" listicles without critical substance
- Commercial digital illustration, graphic design, UX — unless explicitly art-framed

### Soft exclusions (require judgment — default to include if uncertain)
- Mainstream tech coverage of AI that doesn't engage with named artists or practices
- Architecture using digital tools instrumentally — check: is there critical intent?
- Academic papers outside art/technology intersection
- Gaming coverage — include if there is a named artist, modder, or art-framing
- Fan art — generally exclude unless it surfaces a community with its own critical discourse
- Vaporwave, meme art, internet aesthetics — include if the piece engages critically
  with the practice rather than just cataloguing it

## Source Quality

### Tier 1 — Preferred (high confidence)
Art Basel editorial (artbasel.com/stories), Rhizome, Creative Applications Network,
We Make Money Not Art, Neural.it, Ars Electronica, ZKM, V&A Research,
Serpentine, Tate Research, TOPLAP, Processing Foundation, ISEA proceedings,
DAM Berlin, Eyeo Festival, fxhash blog, Art Blocks blog, Transmediale

### Tier 2 — Caution (surface selectively, check for substance)
Hyperallergic, The Art Newspaper, Artforum, Frieze, Wired (Design section),
MIT Media Lab blog, Dezeen (when digital art is primary subject),
Vice Arts, Colossal, It's Nice That, Creative Boom

### Tier 3 — Community sources (high signal for non-institutional track)
Pouet.net, Demozoo, Shadertoy community posts, Hackaday, Adafruit blog,
Lines (llllllll.co — modular synthesis / creative technology),
Lurk (experimental music and technology), NIME proceedings,
Algorave.com, livecode.world, GitHub READMEs for artist-tools,
Itch.io game-as-art releases, Bandcamp net label releases

### Excluded
SEO aggregators, press release wires (PR Newswire, BusinessWire),
crypto/NFT trading platforms, AI hype blogs with no art focus,
"best digital art" listicles, stock illustration platforms

## Relevance Thresholds

**High relevance — always include:**
Named practitioner from any part of the field map; institutional commission or major
exhibition; new tool built for or by artists; critical writing from Tier 1 sources;
historical documentation of pre-2000 digital art; any signal that names the tension
between institutional and non-institutional tracks explicitly

**Marginal — use judgment:**
General AI art coverage without practitioner focus; tech art that is decorative or
purely commercial; single-mention practitioners with no other signal context;
festival coverage without named artists or works

**Out of scope:**
Anything on the hard exclusion list; digital illustration or design not framed
as art practice; traditional art with digitally produced catalogue images only

---

## Deduplication

- Same URL from multiple sources: keep first, discard subsequent
- Same story across multiple outlets: keep highest-tier source
- Event announcement + review of same event: keep both — different signal types
- Updated versions of same work: keep if substantively new information
- Practitioner already in graph: still surface new signals — the graph deepens, not just widens

---

## The Central Editorial Question

Before passing any signal, ask:
*Is there a named human (or collective) making intentional creative decisions
using digital technology as essential to the work?*

If yes — surface it, regardless of whether it has institutional recognition.
The scout does not police the boundary of art. It maps what practitioners are actually doing.
